# Southeast Asia

## Authority on paper isn't authority in the room

Here's a pattern I keep noticing. The org chart tells you who's supposed to decide. The room tells you who actually does. They're usually not the same people.

A few years ago, I was in a fintech deal that had been stuck for a year. Everyone assumed someone was blocking it. Turns out nobody was. Legal and compliance just had different, unwritten ideas of what "finished" meant, and those two ideas had been quietly running side by side the whole time, never actually talking to each other.

Funnily enough, my MBA dissertation at Warwick ran into the exact same shape, in a completely different setting. I was looking at why companies in Malaysia and Singapore drag their feet on adopting renewable energy procurement, even when the numbers clearly work in their favour. Turns out it's rarely a flat no. It's usually just "this is how we've always done it", which is a much harder thing to notice, let alone change.

A stalled fintech deal and a slow renewables decision have nothing to do with each other on the surface. But I keep seeing the same thing show up: the real holdup usually isn't a decision anyone made on purpose. It's just a process that's gone quiet and unquestioned.

## Consistent throughput, adaptable process

At one point, we were entering Malaysia, Singapore, and Indonesia as three separate markets, treating each one as its own standalone push. Looking back, the bigger win was sitting one level up. If we'd unified the three under a single entry strategy, with room for cultural adaptation in each, every market would have shown up as a contributing piece of one larger goal, instead of three smaller ones running in parallel and never quite compounding.

The lesson sits one level deeper than fragmentation. The goal and the standard of execution have to stay constant across markets. The process for getting there can't. Rigid and the whole thing breaks against local reality. Loose and nothing compounds across markets. Capital markets, biotech, business turnarounds, the same tension shows up everywhere I've worked.

## Southeast Asia is one market, not eleven

That's the thing about this region. It's never really one market. It's a collective of many countries, Thailand, Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia, and others across Southeast Asia, each with its own pace, its own culture, its own version of "this is how it's done." Looked at individually, they're a string of mid-sized economies. Looked at together, the region is home to roughly 700 million people, on par with the population of the European Union, and big enough to rank among the world's largest economies if counted as one.

It's a similar idea to how the United States gets approached. Nobody builds fifty different market entries for fifty states. Companies build one national strategy, then flex it state by state for local regulation, taste, and culture. Southeast Asia rewards the same instinct, treated as one opportunity with the throughput to match, not eleven separate small bets that never quite add up.

Once you know to look for it, market entry across Southeast Asia gets a lot easier to read.
